Abstract

Robotic surface-interaction tasks, such as spray painting or welding, require both accurate geometric planning and precise motion execution. While modern motion planners generate valid geometric paths, they often lack the expert motor patterns observed in human operators. Conversely, learning from demonstration often tightly couples task execution to the specific training geometry, limiting transferability. We propose a modular framework that decouples geometric motion planning from execution-level expertise. Expert behavior is represented as a vocabulary of interpretable, atomic motor rules — such as velocity scaling and orientation offsets — that systematically modify a geometrically planned reference path. We train a multimodal neural network to infer rule parameters jointly from kinematic trajectory data and CAD model geometry. We evaluate our approach through dynamic simulation on L-shaped and window-shaped objects, demonstrating that the model successfully extracts velocity and orientation rules across both topologies.

Method

Rule-Based Skill Representation

Expert skills are encoded as a structured vocabulary of parametric transformations. A raw geometric path is decomposed into a sequence of spatial segments (straight edges, corners). Each rule ρk consists of an activation condition evaluated against segment geometry and a dynamic override parameterised by θk. The current vocabulary includes two core rules: a velocity scaling rule that applies a continuous speed multiplier (range 0.1–3.0×), and an orientation offset rule that introduces a systematic tool tilt (quaternion offset) relative to the surface normal.

Trajectory Refinement via Dynamic Simulation

To produce physically continuous trajectories, a pure-pursuit controller tracks a lookahead pose along the reference path. The resulting wrench is integrated via RK4, modelling the end-effector as a rigid body with mass, inertia, and viscous damping. Rules are injected as force and torque modifiers, so the generated trajectories exhibit smooth momentum and continuous velocities — matching what a real robot would produce.

Multimodal Rule Inference Network

The model takes two inputs: a kinematic trajectory and the corresponding CAD mesh. Trajectory orientations are mapped to a continuous 6D rotation representation, giving 10-dimensional per-timestep features (Δt, linear velocity 3D, rotation 6D). These are processed by a 1D convolutional encoder with masked average pooling. The CAD model is sampled to a point cloud and encoded by a PointNet++ encoder. Both embeddings are concatenated and passed through a shared MLP with two output heads: a regression head for continuous rule parameters and a classification head for segment type (straight / corner / none).

Transfer to Unseen Geometries

At inference time, a sliding window with SVD-based curvature analysis automatically labels each segment of a new, unseen path as straight or corner — no manual annotation required. The inferred rules are then injected into their corresponding segment types during simulation, yielding a refined trajectory that preserves geometric structure while incorporating the expert's motor strategies.

Results

We evaluate rule classification and parameter regression on two simulated datasets: L-shaped geometries (10,000 trajectories, 8k/1k/1k split) and window-frame geometries (1,000 trajectories, 800/100/100 split). Test sets use out-of-distribution parameter ranges to assess generalisation.
